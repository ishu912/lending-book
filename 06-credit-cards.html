6  Credit Card Economics

Credit cards are arguably the most sophisticated retail financial product ever designed. Understanding their economics helps you understand why all other lending products are priced the way they are.

6.1 Why Credit Cards Are the Best Lending Product

From a bank CEO’s perspective:

Metric Credit Cards
IRR Very high
ROTA High
Capital efficiency Excellent
Cross-sell Massive
Stickiness Extreme
Merchant dependency Low
Data richness Best-in-class

6.1.1 The Revenue Stack

Credit cards generate income from multiple simultaneous streams:

  1. Interchange/MDR (~1.8–2.5% per transaction) — the merchant’s cost
  2. Revolver interest (36–42% APR on outstanding balances)
  3. Annual/joining fees (₹500–₹5,000+)
  4. Late payment fees (₹500–₹1,200 per instance)
  5. Foreign exchange markup (2–3.5%)
  6. EMI conversion fees (processing charges on card EMI)
  7. Cash advance fees (2.5–3% + 42% interest)
  8. Insurance and add-on products (sold via card relationship)

No other lending product generates this many revenue lines from a single customer relationship.

6.2 The Transactor-Revolver Subsidy

This is the single most important concept in credit card economics.

6.2.1 Two Types of Card Users

Transactors (~60–70% of users): Pay full balance every month. They use the free 45-day credit period and pay nothing.

Revolvers (~30–40%): Carry a balance month to month. They pay 36–42% APR on outstanding amounts.

6.2.2 The Cross-Subsidy

Transactors are essentially getting a free ride. The bank earns almost nothing from them directly (just MDR from the merchant). But the bank doesn’t mind because:

  • Revolvers generate massive interest income
  • This income subsidizes the cost of serving transactors
  • Transactors provide transaction volume that justifies merchant MDR
  • Some transactors eventually become revolvers

This self-selection mechanism is powerful: customers choose their own pricing through their behavior. Banks don’t need to predict perfectly — they let behavior reveal willingness to pay.

6.2.3 Why This Matters for EMI Comparisons

In no-cost EMI / checkout finance, there is no revolver subsidy. Every customer pays 0%, and the only income is subvention. This is why EMI subvention must be higher than card MDR — there’s no cross-subsidy to lean on.

6.3 Why Card APR Is Higher Than Personal Loan APR

This seems contradictory — if cards are such great products, why is the APR so much higher?

6.3.1 Open-Ended vs Closed-Ended Credit

Dimension Personal Loan Credit Card
Principal Fixed Revolving
Tenure Fixed No fixed end
Repayment Mandatory amortization Optional (minimum due)
Exposure Decreases monthly Can stay high indefinitely
Duration control Bank controls Customer controls

Open-ended credit is inherently riskier — the bank cannot predict how long the exposure will last or when (if ever) the customer will pay down.

6.3.2 Higher APR Reflects Higher Optionality

Credit card APR prices in:

  1. Uncertainty of duration — bank doesn’t know when you’ll repay
  2. Revolving risk — balance can grow, not just shrink
  3. Behavioral unpredictability — usage patterns vary wildly
  4. Operational cost — managing millions of small daily transactions
  5. Rewards/cashback cost — partially funded from revolver interest

A personal loan has predictable cashflows. A credit card does not. Higher uncertainty = higher price.

6.4 MDR as Subvention for the Free Credit Period

Here’s an insight that connects cards to checkout finance:

MDR is essentially the subvention for the 45-day interest-free credit period.

The logic:

Checkout Finance Credit Card
Merchant pays subvention Merchant pays MDR
Customer gets 0% EMI Customer gets 45-day free credit
Lender earns from subvention Issuer earns from MDR (interchange)
Tenure: 3–12 months Tenure: ~45 days
Subvention: 5–10% MDR: ~2%

Same mechanism, different product. MDR is lower because:

  • Much shorter tenure (45 days vs 6 months) → extreme IRR on short float → low take-rate needed
  • Lower credit risk on transactors (near-zero loss on free period)
  • Revolver cross-subsidy cushions the economics
  • Cross-selling income from the card relationship

6.5 Losses Are Controllable Despite Being Unsecured

Credit cards are unsecured, yet losses are manageable because:

  • Minimum due ensures partial cashflow even from struggling customers
  • Limits can be reduced dynamically based on behavior
  • Spends can be blocked instantly (real-time risk management)
  • Collections start early — no 90-day wait like term loans
  • Bureau pressure is strong — customers protect their score
  • Cards are tied to salary/bank accounts — natural touchpoints

High IRR absorbs losses easily. Even at 4–6% loss rates, the 36%+ APR from revolvers makes the economics work.

6.6 Why Cards Create Habit, Not Just Usage

Once someone starts using a card:

  • It becomes the default payment method
  • It’s saved in every online checkout
  • Switching cost is psychological, not technical
  • It creates sticky, predictable spend patterns
  • Customer lifetime can be 10–20+ years

Most loans are one-off events. Cards are ongoing relationships. This massively boosts customer lifetime value.

NoteMemorize This

Credit cards work because they monetize time, behavior, and optionality — not just interest. Revolvers subsidize transactors, merchants fund the free credit period, and the multi-stream revenue model makes cards the most profitable retail banking product.
