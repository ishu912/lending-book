5  Banks vs NBFCs: Two Worlds of Lending

There’s an apparent contradiction in lending: some lenders love short-tenure loans while others hate them. The resolution lies in understanding two fundamentally different business models.

5.1 World 1: Capital-Constrained Lenders

NBFCs, Fintechs, BNPL, Consumer Durable Lenders

Their core strategy:

Use money → Get it back fast → Lend again → Churn capital → Multiply return

5.1.1 Why They Love Short Tenure

  • Capital comes back quickly → can lend the same money multiple times per year
  • IRR becomes very high
  • Small absolute profit × many cycles = big annual return
  • Lower credit risk (short exposure window)
  • Losses hit early → limited downside
  • Perfect for working-capital-funded lenders

5.1.2 Examples

  • Bajaj Finance CD loans (3–6 month EMI)
  • BNPL lenders (15–30 days)
  • Merchant cash advance with daily repayment
  • KreditBee, CASHe, ZestMoney short-term loans

5.1.3 Their Optimization Function

\[\text{ROA} = \text{Capital Turnover} \times \text{Yield per Turn}\]

5.2 World 2: Liability-Rich Lenders

Banks, Housing Finance Companies, Large CASA Institutions

Their core strategy:

Deploy large balance sheet slowly → Collect stable interest income for years

5.2.1 Why They Love Long Tenure

  • They don’t need capital churn — they have huge cheap deposits (CASA at 4–6%)
  • They want to earn interest for 5–20 years
  • Early prepayment cuts total interest income
  • Long loans = relationship lock-in (home loan → salary account → credit card → insurance)
  • Asset-liability matching favors long durations

5.2.2 Examples

  • Home loans (10–30 years)
  • Auto loans (5–7 years)
  • Personal loans (2–5 years)
  • Education loans, MSME term loans

5.2.3 Their Optimization Function

\[\text{Profit} = \text{NII (Net Interest Income)} \times \text{Loan Duration}\]

5.3 Side-by-Side Comparison

Dimension NBFC/Fintech Bank
Loves Short tenure Long tenure
Why Higher IRR, fast capital recycling More total interest income
Goal Maximize ROA/IRR Maximize NII
Hates Money locked for long Early repayment
Cost of funds 8–12% (market borrowing) 4–6% (CASA deposits)

Risk appetite High (priced for it) Moderate (seeks stability)

5.4 The Framework to Remember

ImportantThe Key Framework
  • If lender is capital-scarce → they want high IRR → short tenure is king
  • If lender is deposit-rich → they want high total interest → long tenure is king

This is why these behaviors appear contradictory but are both rational.

5.5 Where Does Checkout Finance Fit?

Checkout finance → consumer durable loans → 3–6 months

Who does this?

  • NBFCs (Bajaj, TVS Credit)
  • Consumer finance arms of banks
  • Fintech-NBFC tie-ups

These operate like capital-constrained lenders even when they’re subsidiaries of banks. They love short tenure, fast repayments, high IRR, small loans, and high churn.

Banks participate because of merchant relationships, but NBFCs dominate because the model fits their DNA.

5.6 Why Banks Don’t Chase Small Consumer Loans

Even though checkout finance earns 22–28% IRR, banks largely stay away. Here’s why:

5.6.1 1. Terrible Unit Economics

Banks aren’t built for ₹5K–₹50K, 3–6 month loans. Their fixed cost per loan (KYC, underwriting, LOS, support, reconciliation, collections) is similar for a ₹5,000 loan and a ₹10 lakh loan. Cost per rupee lent becomes very high on small tickets.

5.6.2 2. Capital Deployment Mismatch

Banks sit on huge CASA deposits at 4–6%. Their problem isn’t scarcity of capital — it’s finding long-term deployment. Short-tenure loans return capital too fast and produce very little total interest.

5.6.3 3. No Pricing Power

In no-cost EMI, the customer pays 0% and the bank earns only through subvention. Banks don’t control risk pricing — merchants negotiate hard. Banks take credit risk for limited reward.

5.6.4 4. Weak Collections Infrastructure

Banks don’t run door-knocking, POS-based agents, last-mile field collections for ₹2,000 overdues, or high-frequency micro-collections. NBFCs do.

5.6.5 5. Regulatory Burden

Bank KYC/compliance costs are higher. Every small loan must meet the same reporting standards as a large loan.

5.6.6 6. Cultural Mismatch

Banks are relationship-driven, long-tenure institutions. The checkout finance model requires speed, automation, and tolerance for high volume with thin margins.

NoteMemorize This

Banks hate early repayment because it kills interest income. NBFCs love short tenure because it boosts IRR and capital turns. Both are correct — different economics, different design.
