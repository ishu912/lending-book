9  Banks vs NBFCs: Two Worlds of Lending

There’s a puzzle in lending — some lenders love short-tenure loans while others hate them. Both are being rational. The difference is their business model.

9.1 Capital-Scarce Lenders (NBFCs, Fintechs)

NBFCs and fintechs borrow expensive money. Every rupee they borrow costs them something. So their strategy is elegant: lend → get money back fast → lend again → multiply returns on that same capital.

Think of it like a street vendor with a cart. If you have ₹10,000 in cash, you want to turn it twice a day, not once a week. Turn it fast, keep margins tight, but do volume.

They love short tenure because capital turns faster. A ₹10,000 loan at 24% IRR, repaid in 3 months, lets you redeploy that capital 4 times per year. Same ₹10,000, four shots at 24% IRR.

Examples everywhere: Bajaj CD loans (9-12 months), BNPL (3-6 months), merchant cash advance (repaid in 3-4 months out of daily sales).

The optimization formula is simple:

\[\text{ROA} = \text{Capital Turnover} \times \text{Yield per Turn}\] \[\text{Annual ROA} = \frac{1}{\text{Tenure (in years)}} \times \text{IRR}\]

So a 24% IRR on a 3-month loan gives ROA = 4 × 24% = 96%. On a 12-month loan? ROA = 1 × 24% = 24%. Same IRR, but you get four times the return on assets by doing it faster.

9.2 Deposit-Rich Lenders (Banks)

Banks have a completely different problem. They sit on cheap CASA deposits — Savings and Current Accounts. Depositors pay close to 0% to 2% interest. Banks have mountains of this capital.

Their strategy is opposite: deploy large balance sheet slowly, let customers pay EMIs for years, collect stable interest income across decades. They love long tenure because that maximizes total interest earned.

Here’s why. A ₹50 lakh home loan at 9% annual rate:

  • 20-year tenure: Total interest paid ≈ ₹1.4 crore
  • 5-year prepayment: Total interest paid ≈ ₹40 lakh
  • Lost interest: ₹1.4 crore − ₹40 lakh = ₹1.0 crore foregone

That’s why banks charge prepayment penalties. They’re protecting the interest stream.

The optimization is:

\[\text{Profit} = \text{NII} \times \text{Loan Duration}\]

Where NII is Net Interest Income. More duration = more total interest = more profit. The tenure is the multiplier.

Note

A bank’s nightmare: a customer who borrows ₹50 lakh and prepays in 2 years. They’ve locked in a 9% cost of deposits but earn only 2 years of interest instead of 20. That spread got compressed from ₹1.4 crore to ₹8 lakh.

9.3 Side-by-Side Comparison

What They Love What They Hate Cost of Funds Capital Source Risk Appetite Optimization Metric
NBFCs Short tenure (3-12 mo) Long tenure (24+ mo) 10-14% Borrowings (NCDs, repo) Capital turnover × IRR
Banks Long tenure (5+ years) Early repayment 2-4% Deposits, balance sheet NII × duration

9.4 Where Checkout Finance Fits

Checkout finance — “buy now, pay in 3 months” — is a capital-scarce game. Even when a bank subsidiary runs it, it operates like an NBFC. Capital constraint is real.

Why? The checkout use case requires instant approval, instant funding to merchant, and monthly EMI collection from customer. That’s capital churn. You can’t make it work with a 20-year loan mindset.

NBFCs dominate checkout finance because the model fits their DNA. They’re built to borrow expensively, deploy quickly, get capital back fast, and repeat. Banks’ deposit-based model fights this business.

9.5 Why Banks Don’t Chase Small Consumer Loans

Banks could technically enter checkout finance. Most have. But they don’t push hard. Five reasons:

  1. Terrible unit economics on small tickets. A ₹5,000 loan and a ₹50,000 loan cost almost the same to underwrite, approve, and service. Margins get razor-thin. NBFCs can live with 1-2% net margins because they move volume and turn capital. Banks can’t.

  2. Capital deployment mismatch. Banks have ₹50 crore to deploy. They’d rather make one ₹50 crore home loan than 10,000 ₹5,000 checkout loans. Same upfront work, but the big loan funds faster.

  3. No pricing power in no-cost EMI. Checkout finance is sold as “no cost.” The subvention comes from the merchant. The bank can’t raise the interest rate. So yield is capped. That doesn’t work for capital-intensive operations.

  4. Weak collections for small overdues. If a customer is 10 days late on a ₹50 lakh home loan, the bank’s collection calls matter. For a ₹5,000 loan, the cost of collection exceeds recovery. You swallow the loss.

  5. Regulatory burden is the same. A ₹5,000 consumer loan and a ₹10 lakh consumer loan need the same RBI compliance, the same credit bureau reporting, the same regulatory capital. So the cost-to-serve per rupee deployed is much higher on the small loan.

Important

Banks and NBFCs aren’t being irrational about tenure preferences. They’re optimizing for different constraints. A bank that chases short-tenure small loans is destroying shareholder value. An NBFC that locks capital in long-tenure loans is doing the same. Each is right for their model.

9.6 Memorize This

Banks hate early repayment because it kills interest income. NBFCs love short tenure because it boosts IRR and capital turns. Different economics, same rationality.
