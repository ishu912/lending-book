10  Negotiation Playbook & Cheat Sheets

Everything in this book converges here. This chapter gives you the tools to negotiate lending economics — whether you’re a fintech negotiating with lenders, a merchant negotiating subvention, or a product manager designing pricing.

10.1 The 30-Second Negotiation Script

When someone quotes you an IRR or subvention number, here’s how to verify it instantly:

Step 1: Ask: “What IRR are you targeting?”

Step 2: If they say ~24%, compute mentally:

\[\text{Take\%} \approx (R - F) \times \frac{n+1}{24} + \varepsilon + L\]

Step 3: Plug numbers: \(24\% \times (6+1)/24 = 7\%\) → \(+1\%\) opex \(+2\%\) loss = \(10\%\) → minus float \(1.7\%\) → ~8.3%

Step 4: State: “So we should be looking at ~8–9% for 6-month no-cost EMI to make economics work.”

That short script converts portfolio talk into per-transaction % instantly.

10.2 Quick Reference: Subvention by Tenure

For 24% IRR target, 1% opex, 2% loss, no float:

Tenure Formula Approximate Take-Rate
1 month \((1+1)\% + 3\%\) ~5%
3 months \((3+1)\% + 3\%\) ~7%
6 months \((6+1)\% + 3\%\) ~10%
9 months \((9+1)\% + 3\%\) ~13%
12 months \((12+1)\% + 3\%\) ~16%

With 6% float, subtract ~\(F \times (n+1)/24\) from each.

10.3 Key Questions to Ask Lenders

When a lender quotes a subvention rate, push them on:

  1. “What is your assumed lifetime loss rate?” — If they say 5% for a 6-month product, challenge it. Industry is 2–3%.

  2. “Is that realized or buffered?” — Lenders sometimes pad loss assumptions. Ask for actual portfolio data.

  3. “What is your cost of funds benchmark?” — If they’re an NBFC at 9–10% cost of funds, their economics are different from a bank at 5%.

  4. “What average outstanding assumption are you using?” — This reveals how they’re modeling capital deployment.

  5. “What IRR does this imply?” — Back-calculate their IRR from their subvention ask. If it’s above 28–30%, they’re extracting excess margin.

  6. “What alternative deployment yields do you have?” — If their next-best use of capital earns 14%, they should accept anything above that.

10.4 How to Challenge Inflated Subvention Asks

10.4.1 Red Flag 1: Linear Math

If someone says: “24% IRR ÷ 12 months = 2% per month × 6 months = 12% subvention”

Your response: “That ignores amortization. The lender gets principal back every month, so capital at risk reduces. True subvention for 6 months at 24% IRR is ~8–9%, not 12%.”

10.4.2 Red Flag 2: Ignoring Float

If the lender quotes subvention without accounting for float:

Your response: “At scale, you earn 5–7% float on pooled repayments. That reduces required subvention by ~1.5–2% for a 6-month product.”

10.4.3 Red Flag 3: Inflated Loss Assumptions

If they claim 5% loss on a 6-month checkout EMI:

Your response: “Industry benchmarks for 6-month consumer durable EMI are 2–3%. Bajaj Finance runs at 1.5–2.5% credit cost. Where is the extra 2–3% going?”

10.4.4 Red Flag 4: Monthly Loss Quoting

If someone divides loss by 12 or treats it like APR:

Your response: “Loss % is lifetime per cohort, not monthly. 3% loss doesn’t mean 3% per month — that would be 36% annual, which would bankrupt any lender.”

10.4.5 Red Flag 5: Tenure-Flat Subvention

If a lender asks for the same subvention across all tenures:

Your response: “Required subvention grows with tenure. A flat 8% works for 6 months but massively overpays for 3 months. We need tenure-tiered pricing.”

10.5 Component-Based Negotiation Framework

Instead of negotiating a single subvention number, break it into components:

\[\text{Subvention} = \text{Funding Drag} + \text{Loss} + \text{Opex} + \text{Fair Margin}\]

Component Typical Range What to Challenge
Funding drag \((R-F) \times (n+1)/24\) Push on float and cost of funds
Loss provision 1.5–4% Demand actual portfolio data
Operating cost 0.5–2% Push on automation and scale
Fair margin 1–3% Negotiate based on volume commitment

Anything above the sum of these components is negotiation surplus — pure extra margin the lender is extracting.

10.6.3 Key Principles

  1. IRR is a capital efficiency metric — not a revenue metric
  2. Cost of funds determines baseline economics — banks at 5% vs NBFCs at 10%
  3. Duration drives funding drag — longer tenure = higher subvention
  4. Loss drives subvention sensitivity — loss assumptions matter more than IRR target
  5. Credit cards succeed due to short float + revolver cross-subsidy
  6. BNPL must price risk via merchant subsidy (no revolver income)
  7. Minimum ticket rules are IRR constraints in disguise
  8. Subvention negotiation should be component-based, not IRR-based

10.6.4 Decision Framework

If you are… Focus on…
GMV/growth focused Approval rates and conversion lift
Cost focused Component-based subvention breakdown
Building for scale Float optimization and loss reduction
Multi-lender platform Tenure-tiered pricing and lender competition
