6  Float, Fees, and Down Payments

Subvention is not the only way a lender makes money on a merchant lending deal. There are three other levers: float, processing fees, and down payments. This chapter shows you how each one works and how they combine to reduce what you actually have to pay.

6.1 What Is Float?

Float is money sitting idle in someone’s account before it needs to go somewhere else.

When you deposit ₹1,000 in your savings account and it earns 4% interest per year, you’re earning on float. The bank takes your money, deploys it elsewhere at higher rates, and gives you 4%. That 4% is the value of float.

In merchant lending, float works the same way. EMI money pools in accounts, refunds accumulate, brand subsidy funds sit waiting. The lender invests this idle cash in short-term money market instruments (bonds, reverse repos, T-bills) earning 5-8% per year.

This float income reduces the subvention the lender needs from you.

6.2 Where Float Comes From

EMI Collections Pooling

Customers pay EMIs on, say, the 10th of each month. But the lender doesn’t immediately deduct from their account or deploy the money. The cash sits in a pooling account earning interest for 1-3 days before it’s reconciled against the loan ledger.

₹1 crore in EMI collections earning 6% float for 2 days = ₹1,00,00,000 × 0.06 ÷ 365 × 2 = ₹32,877 of free money.

Refund Pools

When a customer returns a product (within 30 days for appliances, 10 days for electronics), the refund goes into a pool. It sits for 2-7 days before it’s matched to a customer account and reversed.

If the NBFC has ₹50 lakh in refunds sitting for an average of 4 days: ₹50,00,000 × 0.06 ÷ 365 × 4 = ₹32,877.

Staggered Disbursements

Some lenders disburse in stages. They approve for ₹30,000 but release ₹10,000 at purchase, ₹10,000 at delivery, ₹10,000 at final inspection. Money that was supposed to be out sits earning float between tranches.

Brand Subsidy Pools

When a brand (like Samsung or LG) subsidizes EMI schemes, they deposit funds upfront. The lender invests this until it’s deployed against actual loans. Brand deposit of ₹1 crore at 7% earning float for 2 months = ₹1,00,00,000 × 0.07 ÷ 12 × 2 = ₹11.67 lakhs.

6.3 Does T+1 Settlement Create Float?

No. People often confuse merchant settlement timing with float. Let me clarify.

Scenario 1: T+0 Settlement, No Float

  • Day 1: Customer buys for ₹30,000 EMI
  • Day 1: Lender disburses ₹28,500 to merchant (net of subvention)
  • Day 1: Customer makes first EMI payment (₹5,000)
  • Day 1: Lender deducts ₹5,000 from the loan
  • Result: No float. Money moved immediately.

Scenario 2: T+1 Settlement with Float

  • Day 1: Customer buys for ₹30,000 EMI
  • Day 1: Lender disburses ₹28,500 to merchant
  • Day 1: Customer makes first EMI payment (₹5,000)
  • Day 2: EMI payment clears and is posted to the loan
  • Result: Still no float from settlement timing. The T+1 delay is just about ledger posting, not about earning on idle money.

Scenario 3: T+1 Settlement WITH Float (Pooling)

  • Day 1: Customer buys for ₹30,000 EMI
  • Day 1-15: Many customers make payments throughout the month
  • Day 15: Lender has ₹50 lakh in unposted EMI payments sitting in a transaction account
  • Day 16-17: These payments are reconciled, netted, and posted to loans
  • Result: ₹50 lakh earned float for 1-2 days while sitting idle.

The float comes from batching, not from T+1 settlement itself. T+1 is about when you get paid. Float is about how long money sits in the lender’s account earning interest.

6.4 How Float Reduces Subvention

Every percentage point of float yield saves you roughly 0.3-0.5% in subvention, depending on tenure.

Here’s why: in a 6-month EMI, the lender has money outstanding for an average of 3.5 months. If they’re earning 6% float on average balances for 3.5 months, that’s:

Float income = Average balance × 6% × (3.5/12) = Average balance × 1.75%

Since average balance on a ₹30,000 loan is ₹15,000, float income is roughly ₹262. That’s worth about 0.87% of the original ₹30,000 principal. So 6% float yield saves roughly 0.87% in subvention on a 6-month loan.

Tenure Float Yield → Subvention Savings
1-month 6% float → saves ~0.5% subvention
3-month 6% float → saves ~1.0% subvention
6-month 6% float → saves ~1.75% subvention
12-month 6% float → saves ~3.0% subvention

A 6-month loan that would need 10% subvention at 0% float only needs 8.25% subvention if the lender earns 6% float.

This is why lenders obsess over float. It’s free money. It improves unit economics without touching the merchant economics.

6.5 Processing Fees

Many lenders charge the customer an upfront processing fee: 1-3% of the loan amount.

This is separate from subvention (paid by merchant) and is instead paid directly by the customer.

Example:

  • Loan amount: ₹30,000
  • Processing fee: 2% = ₹600
  • Subvention needed (before fee): 9%
  • Subvention needed (after fee): 8%

The ₹600 processing fee is worth about 2% of the loan, which directly offsets the subvention need. If you charge the customer 2% and reduce subvention by 2%, the lender’s IRR stays the same but the merchant cost drops.

The GST Nuance:

Processing fees are taxable. On a ₹600 fee, GST is 18%: ₹108.

So the customer pays ₹708, and the lender keeps ₹600 after remitting ₹108 to the government. Net to lender = ₹600, but customer sees ₹708.

From the merchant’s perspective, if the lender says “we’ll charge the customer 3% processing fee instead of asking you for 3% subvention,” the merchant sees a win (no cost to them). The lender still gets the rupees they need, just from a different pocket.

6.6 Down Payments

A down payment reduces the financed amount. If a customer buys a ₹30,000 TV and puts down ₹9,000 (30%), the lender only finances ₹21,000.

Why This Matters for Subvention:

Subvention is calculated as a percentage of the financed amount, not the total purchase price.

Subvention without down payment: ₹30,000 × 8% = ₹2,400 Subvention with 30% down: ₹21,000 × 8% = ₹1,680

Simple rule:

\[\text{Subvention(with down payment)} = \text{Subvention(no down)} \times (1 - \text{Down%})\]

A 20% down payment reduces subvention by roughly 20%.

Why Merchants and Lenders Like Down Payments:

  1. It shrinks the financed amount, so less subvention is needed
  2. Customer has skin in the game — they paid their own money upfront — so defaults drop sharply
  3. If the customer defaults, the down payment is the lender’s cushion

A customer who paid ₹9,000 down is much less likely to default on a ₹21,000 EMI than someone who financed the full ₹30,000.

Research from Indian fintech lenders shows default rates drop 30-50% when a down payment is required, because the customer is committed.

6.7 The Complete Picture

These three levers — float, fees, down payments — all reduce merchant cost without cutting lender profitability.

Lever What It Does Effect on Merchant
Float Lender earns 5-8% on idle money Reduces subvention by 1-3% depending on tenure
Processing Fee Customer pays 1-3% directly Reduces subvention 1-for-1 (after GST adjustment)
Down Payment Shrinks financed amount Reduces subvention payout by same % (and reduces defaults)

Worked Example:

A lender targets 24% IRR on a 6-month EMI product. Base subvention is 10%.

Now add three optimizations: - 6% float yield saves 1.75% → new subvention need: 8.25% - 2% processing fee on customer → save another 2% → new need: 6.25% - 20% down payment from customer → save 20% payout → final payout = 6.25% × 0.8 = 5%

So a merchant that would have paid ₹3,000 subvention on a ₹30,000 loan now pays only ₹1,050 (5% × ₹21,000 financed). The lender still hits 24% IRR. The customer pays ₹600 processing fee. Everyone is better off.

Note

Memorize This

Float, fees, and down payments are force multipliers on your profitability as a lender. They reduce merchant cost without cutting IRR. This is why Indian fintech lenders have gotten aggressive on all three: charging processing fees, building float management ops, and pushing down payments.
