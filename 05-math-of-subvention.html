5  The Math of Subvention

Subvention is the most misunderstood number in merchant lending. Everyone thinks it’s simple: “6% subvention on a ₹30,000 loan = ₹1,800.” That’s true, but incomplete.

The ₹1,800 has to be worth enough to the lender to give them 22% IRR over six months when they also earn float, charge processing fees, and hold capital. This chapter shows you the math.

We’ll move from the real calculations to a simple formula you can use on the back of an envelope.

5.1 The Cashflows in a No-Cost EMI Loan

Let’s trace a ₹30,000 no-cost EMI loan over six months.

Month 0 (Disbursement): - Lender pays merchant ₹30,000 - Merchant pays subvention S to the lender (e.g., ₹1,500 = 5%) - Lender pays origination/servicing cost εP (let’s say ε = 1%, so ₹300) - Net lender cash out: ₹30,000 - ₹1,500 + ₹300 = ₹28,800

Months 1-6 (Collections): - Customer pays EMI of ₹30,000/6 = ₹5,000 each month

Expected Loss: Sometime around month 3 (the midpoint), the lender will face expected losses. If loss rate = 2%, that’s ₹600 they won’t collect.

Here’s the cashflow table:

Month Customer Payment Lender Receives Notes
0 - -₹28,800 Net disbursement after subvention
1 ₹5,000 ₹5,000
2 ₹5,000 ₹5,000
3 (mid) - -₹600 Expected loss taken
3 ₹5,000 ₹5,000
4 ₹5,000 ₹5,000
5 ₹5,000 ₹5,000
6 ₹5,000 ₹5,000

The lender’s job is to find the subvention S that makes the NPV of this cashflow equal to zero at their required IRR.

5.2 Converting Annual IRR to Monthly

An IRR is always annualized. If the lender needs 24% annual IRR, we need to convert this to a monthly discount rate.

The formula is:

\[r_{\text{monthly}} = (1 + r_{\text{annual}})^{1/12} - 1\]

For 24% annual:

\[r_{\text{monthly}} = (1.24)^{1/12} - 1 = 1.0181 - 1 = 1.81\%\]

So each month, the lender discounts at 1.81%.

Why not just 24% ÷ 12 = 2%? Because interest is compounded. Two percent per month for 12 months gives you \((1.02)^{12} - 1 = 26.8\%\), not 24%. The 1.81% monthly rate properly compounds to 24% annually.

5.3 The Exact Subvention Formula

The lender solves for S such that the Net Present Value of all cashflows is zero.

\[0 = -P(1 - s + \varepsilon) + \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{P/n}{(1 + r)^{t/12}} - L \cdot P\]

Where: - P = principal (₹30,000) - s = take-rate as a decimal (0.05 for 5%) - ε = opex as a decimal (0.01 for 1%) - r = annual IRR (0.24 for 24%) - n = tenure in months (6) - L = loss rate as a decimal (0.02 for 2%) - t = month number

This equation says: “The money I put out today, minus subvention and opex, plus the discounted value of future EMI collections, minus expected losses, equals zero.”

Solving this requires algebra. The good news: someone already did it. The formula for subvention is:

\[s \approx \frac{r}{(1 + r)^{n/12} - 1} \cdot \frac{n + 1}{24} + \varepsilon + L\]

This exact formula is what Bajaj, Flexipay, and every lender uses in their models. But you don’t need to memorize it. The linear approximation below is almost as good.

5.4 The Back-of-Envelope Formula (Memorize This)

Here’s the key insight: in an EMI loan, the lender doesn’t have all the money out for all twelve months. They get it back in chunks.

Average time outstanding = \(\frac{n + 1}{2}\) months

Then we convert from months to years by dividing by 12:

Average time as a fraction of year = \(\frac{n + 1}{24}\)

So:

\[\text{Take%} \approx (R - F) \cdot \frac{n+1}{24} + \varepsilon + L\]

Where: - R = required annual IRR (as a decimal, e.g., 0.24) - F = float yield (typically 4-8%, so 0.05) - n = tenure in months - ε = opex rate - L = loss rate

Let me walk you through why \((n+1)/24\) makes sense.

Why \((n+1)/24\)?

You lend ₹30,000 for 6 months. You get ₹5,000 back each month starting month 1.

  • After month 1: you have ₹25,000 out
  • After month 2: you have ₹20,000 out
  • After month 3: you have ₹15,000 out
  • After month 4: you have ₹10,000 out
  • After month 5: you have ₹5,000 out
  • After month 6: you have ₹0 out

Average = (30 + 25 + 20 + 15 + 10 + 5) / 6 = 17.5k = 3.5 months worth.

For a 6-month loan, average tenure is (6+1)/2 = 3.5 months. Divide by 12 to convert to years: 3.5/12 = 0.292 = (6+1)/24. Exactly.

For a 3-month loan: (3+1)/24 = 4/24 = 1/6 ≈ 1.67 months average. For a 12-month loan: (12+1)/24 = 13/24 ≈ 5.4 months average.

This is why longer tenures need higher subvention. You’re asking the lender to lock up capital for longer.

5.5 Worked Examples

Let’s calculate take-rates for three different tenures. Assumptions: - Required IRR: R = 24% - Float yield: F = 6% - Opex rate: ε = 1% - Loss rate: L = 2%

3-Month Loan:

Exact formula (you’d use a calculator): ~5.2% Back-of-envelope: \((0.24 - 0.06) \times \frac{3+1}{24} + 0.01 + 0.02 = 0.18 \times 0.167 + 0.03 = 0.030 + 0.03 = 6.0\%\)

6-Month Loan:

Exact formula: ~8.8% Back-of-envelope: \((0.24 - 0.06) \times \frac{6+1}{24} + 0.01 + 0.02 = 0.18 \times 0.292 + 0.03 = 0.052 + 0.03 = 8.2\%\)

12-Month Loan:

Exact formula: ~14.5% Back-of-envelope: \((0.24 - 0.06) \times \frac{12+1}{24} + 0.01 + 0.02 = 0.18 \times 0.542 + 0.03 = 0.097 + 0.03 = 12.7\%\)

Notice the back-of-envelope is a bit low for longer tenures. That’s because we used a linear approximation instead of the exact compounding formula. But it’s close enough to sense-check.

5.6 The Instant Cheat Numbers

For a lender targeting 24% IRR with 6% float yield, 1% opex, 2% loss rate:

Tenure Take-rate
1-month ~5%
3-month ~6-7%
6-month ~8-10%
12-month ~14-16%

These numbers show up everywhere in Indian merchant lending. If you see “6-month EMI, 8% merchant discount,” now you know why.

The pattern: For every extra month of tenure, you add roughly 0.3% to the take-rate.

Actually, a simpler cheat: For R = 24%, F = 6%, ε = 1%, L = 2%:

\[\text{Take%} \approx \frac{n+1}{10} + 3\%\]

  • 1-month: (1+1)/10 + 3% = 5.2%
  • 3-month: (3+1)/10 + 3% = 7.0%
  • 6-month: (6+1)/10 + 3% = 10.0%
  • 12-month: (12+1)/10 + 3% = 16.3%

Close enough for a back-of-envelope calc.

5.7 Why Linear Math Always Overestimates

Here’s a common mistake. Someone says:

“24% IRR means 2% per month. For 6 months, that’s 2% × 6 = 12% subvention.”

Wrong. The lender gets principal back monthly, so they’re not actually earning 24% on the full amount for six months.

Here’s the intuition: if you lend ₹30,000 and get ₹5,000 back each month, by month 4 you only have ₹10,000 still deployed. You can’t earn 24% on ₹10,000 when you’re only owed ₹10,000. The math doesn’t work.

The real answer accounts for this: the lender only needs 8-9% subvention on a 6-month EMI to hit 24% IRR, not 12%.

This is why simple “2% per month” estimates are always too high. The true formula includes the recovery of principal, which reduces how much yield the lender needs.

Important

Memorize This

For 6-month EMI loans at 24% IRR with 6% float and typical opex/loss:

\[\text{Take%} \approx (R - F) \cdot \frac{n+1}{24} + \varepsilon + L\]

Use \((n+1)/24\) because the lender gets principal back over time, not all at the end.
