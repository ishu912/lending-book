2  The Math of Lending

This chapter gives you the formulas you need — from exact calculations to back-of-envelope shortcuts you can use in negotiations.

2.1 Setup: Cashflows You Must Model

For each financed transaction (invoice value = \(P\)):

Time Cashflow Direction
\(t = 0\) Lender disburses \(P\) Outflow: \(-P\)
\(t = 0\) Merchant pays subvention \(S\) Inflow: \(+S\)
\(t = 0\) Operating cost \(\varepsilon P\) Outflow: \(-\varepsilon P\)
\(t = 1 \ldots n\) Customer repays \(P/n\) monthly Inflow: \(+P/n\) each month
\(t = m\) Expected credit loss \(L \cdot P\) Loss (modeled at midpoint)

We choose \(S\) so that the lender’s IRR equals their target.

2.2 Converting Annual IRR to Monthly

\[r_{\text{month}} = (1 + r_{\text{annual}})^{1/12} - 1\]

Example: For 24% annual IRR: \(r_{\text{month}} = (1.24)^{1/12} - 1 \approx 1.81\%\) per month.

2.3 The Exact Subvention Formula

Set NPV = 0 at the target monthly discount rate \(r\):

\[\underbrace{-P + S - \varepsilon P}_{t=0} + \underbrace{\sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{P/n}{(1+r)^k}}_{PV \text{ of installments}} - \underbrace{\frac{L \cdot P}{(1+r)^m}}_{\text{discounted loss}} = 0\]

Solving for subvention:

\[S = P - PV_{\text{inst}} + \varepsilon P + \frac{L \cdot P}{(1+r)^m}\]

Per-transaction take-rate:

\[\frac{S}{P} = 1 - \frac{1}{n} \cdot \frac{1 - (1+r)^{-n}}{r} + \varepsilon + \frac{L}{(1+r)^m}\]

2.4 The Back-of-Envelope Formula

For negotiations, you need something you can compute in your head. Here’s the linear approximation (works when monthly rate < 3%):

2.4.1 The Formula to Memorize

\[\text{Take\%} \approx (R - F) \cdot \frac{n+1}{24} + \varepsilon + L\]

Where:

  • \(R\) = annual required yield (e.g., 0.24 for 24%)
  • \(F\) = annual float yield (e.g., 0.06 for 6%)
  • \(n\) = tenure in months
  • \(\varepsilon\) = opex as fraction (e.g., 0.01 for 1%)
  • \(L\) = expected loss fraction (e.g., 0.02 for 2%)

2.4.2 Why \((n+1)/24\)?

This is the average duration of money outstanding, in years. With equal monthly installments, the average time the capital is deployed is \((n+1)/2\) months. Dividing by 12 gives years, hence \((n+1)/24\).

Think of it as: you lend ₹30,000 for 6 months via equal EMIs. You don’t have ₹30,000 deployed for 6 months — you get chunks back every month. On average, your money is deployed for only ~3.5 months.

2.5 Worked Examples

Assumptions: \(P\) = ₹30,000, Loss \(L\) = 2%, Opex \(\varepsilon\) = 1%, Target IRR = 24%.

2.5.1 Using the Exact Formula

Tenure PV of Installments Discounted Loss Required \(S\) Take-Rate
3 months ₹28,947 ₹592 ₹1,932 6.4%
6 months ₹28,189 ₹566 ₹2,680 8.9%
12 months ₹26,752 ₹535 ₹4,087 13.6%

2.5.2 Using the Back-of-Envelope Formula

With \(R\) = 24%, \(F\) = 0 (no float), \(\varepsilon\) = 1%, \(L\) = 2%:

\[\text{Take\%} = 24\% \times \frac{n+1}{24} + 1\% + 2\% = (n+1)\% + 3\%\]

Tenure Calculation Take-Rate
3 months 4% + 3% ~7%
6 months 7% + 3% ~10%
12 months 13% + 3% ~16%

The linear formula slightly overestimates (conservative), which is actually useful in negotiations — it gives you headroom.

Including float at 6% annual: subtract \(F \times (n+1)/24 = 6\% \times 7/24 \approx 1.75\%\)

  • Without float: ~10%
  • With 6% float: ~10% - 1.75% = ~8.25%

2.7 Why Linear Formulas Always Overestimate

A common mistake: “24% IRR → 2% per month → for 12 months = 24% subvention.” This is completely wrong because:

  1. The lender gets principal back every month — capital at risk reduces
  2. Subvention is received upfront — it has maximum time-value
  3. IRR already accounts for amortization — linear math ignores this

Example: Target IRR = 20%, tenure = 12 months.

  • Naive: 20% subvention
  • Actual: ~11% subvention

The actual is almost half the naive estimate. This is because upfront money is worth more than later money, and the rapid amortization amplifies the IRR from a modest-looking upfront percentage.

ImportantNegotiation Leverage

If a counterpart uses linear math to justify a high subvention ask, you can immediately show the math is wrong. True subvention is always lower than “IRR ÷ tenure” estimates.

NoteMemorize This

\[\text{Take\%} \approx (R - F) \cdot \frac{n+1}{24} + \varepsilon + L\]

For R=24%, no float, 1% opex, 2% loss: Take% ≈ \((n+1)\)% + 3%
