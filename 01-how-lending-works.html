1  How Lending Works

1.1 The Lending Business in One Sentence

A lender borrows money cheaply, lends it out at a higher rate, and keeps the difference as profit. The difference is called the spread. It needs to cover losses from defaults and the cost of running the business.

That’s it. Everything in lending flows from this idea.

1.2 Typical Yields on Retail Loans in India

Different loans have different yields because they have different risk. A home loan is less risky than a personal loan, so the lender charges less interest. Here’s what you see in the market:

Loan Type Collateral? Typical Yield
Home Loan Yes (property) 9-10%
Loan Against Property (LAP) Yes (property) 11-13%
Auto Loan (New) Yes (car) 10-12%
Auto Loan (Used) Yes (car) 14-16%
Gold Loan Yes (gold) 10-12%
Personal Loan No 16-24%
Checkout EMI No 16-28%
Credit Card No 36-48%

Why the difference? Collateral. When you borrow for a home and can’t repay, the lender takes the house. When you borrow for a personal loan and can’t repay, the lender has nothing. The lender’s money is at much more risk. So they charge more.

Unsecured loans also last shorter. A personal loan is typically 2-4 years. A home loan is 20 years. The shorter the loan, the faster the lender gets their money back. If something goes wrong, they lose money for shorter period.

Checkout EMIs and credit cards are the riskiest in this list. No collateral. The customer can simply not pay. So yields are high — 20%+ is common.

1.3 What Is Subvention?

Subvention is the interest income that the lender would have earned from the customer, but instead the merchant pays it upfront so the customer gets 0% EMI.

Let’s use a concrete example.

A customer buys a ₹30,000 product at a store and pays in 6 equal installments. If the lender charged the standard 22% annual interest, the customer would pay extra money each month. But the merchant wants to offer 0%.

Here’s what happens: The lender calculates the 22% IRR upfront. This comes to about ₹3,500 in total interest income. Instead of collecting this from the customer, the merchant pays this ₹3,500 to the lender at the time of purchase. The customer then pays ₹5,000 per month for 6 months — exactly the original loan amount divided equally.

The customer sees 0% interest. The lender earned 22% IRR anyway. The merchant paid ₹3,500 for the privilege. Everyone is happy.

This is subvention.

Subvention is a subsidy. The merchant is subsidizing the interest so the customer saves money. Why? Because the merchant wants the sale. They’re willing to pay for the convenience of financing.

The economics are wild. The lender’s IRR stays the same. The customer’s interest rate drops to 0%. The merchant pays the difference. This only works if the merchant’s profit margin on the product is high enough to absorb the cost.

1.4 MDR vs Subvention

Merchants encounter two different finance costs: MDR and subvention. They are not the same.

Feature MDR Subvention
What is it? Merchant Discount Rate. Fee for payment processing. Interest cost for credit financing.
Typical amount 1-2% of transaction 5-10% of loan amount
Applies to Every card transaction Only financing transactions
When paid Immediately Upfront for the full loan period
Who sets it? Card networks (Visa, Mastercard) Individual lenders

MDR is about processing. When a customer swipes a credit card and makes a payment, the merchant pays the card network and bank for processing that payment. ₹100 purchase → ₹98-99 lands in the merchant’s account.

Subvention is about financing. When a customer doesn’t pay the ₹100 today but spreads it over 6 months, the lender finances it. The merchant compensates the lender for the interest cost and risk.

The key difference: credit costs more than processing. A merchant paying 2% for card processing is getting a much better deal than a merchant paying 8% for subvention. Why? Because with a card, the money is in the merchant’s account in 2 seconds. With a loan, the merchant’s customer might not pay for 12 months.

1.5 The Bajaj Finance Model

Bajaj Finance is one of India’s largest lenders in checkout finance. Their economics show how merchant lending really works.

Bajaj earns money from several streams:

  1. Subvention — The merchant pays upfront. This is the biggest source.
  2. Processing fees — The customer or merchant pays a small fee (~1-2%) to set up the loan.
  3. Late fees — If the customer misses a payment, Bajaj charges a late fee. This is 2-4% per month.
  4. Cross-sell — Bajaj markets credit cards and insurance to customers who get a loan. Extra revenue.
  5. Float — Bajaj takes customer’s money at the beginning of the month (with salary), but the customer’s bills are due in the middle. Bajaj earns interest on this money for 2 weeks.

The point: a lender in merchant lending isn’t just earning interest. They’re earning fees, late fees, and income from investments. The subvention is the core, but it’s not the only lever.

NoteMemorize This

Lending is about the spread. A lender borrows money at one rate and lends at another. The difference, minus losses and costs, is profit. Subvention is not free money for lenders — it’s payment for the interest that the merchant asked them to forgive.
