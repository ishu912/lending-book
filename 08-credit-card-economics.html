8  Credit Card Economics

Credit cards are a different animal than EMI loans. They’re like no-cost EMI on steroids: multiple revenue streams, revolving credit, behavioral unpredictability. This chapter shows you why card pricing looks different and how the economics actually work.

8.1 Why Credit Cards Are Special

A credit card generates revenue from five separate sources. No other lending product does this.

Revenue Stream How It Works Typical Yield
Interchange Percentage of transaction value paid by merchant’s bank to card issuer 0.5-2% of transaction value
Interest on Revolvers APR charged on customers who carry balances (36-42% APR) 3-5% of portfolio annually
Annual Fees ₹500-2,000 per card per year 0.5-1% of portfolio
Late Payment Fees ₹100-500 per missed payment 0.5-1.5% of portfolio
Forex and Cash Advance Fees 1-3% on international transactions and cash withdrawals 0.5% of portfolio

A typical card issuer earns maybe 1-2% interchange, 2-3% revolver interest, and 0.5% in other fees = 3.5-5.5% portfolio yield.

Sounds low. But card issuers also benefit from behavioral economics (customers spend more with cards) and a portfolio effect where profitable revolvers subsidize unprofitable transactors.

8.2 Transactors vs Revolvers

Credit cards split customers into two buckets:

Transactors (60-70% of cardholders): - Pay the full bill every month - Generate only interchange revenue (~1-2% of spend) - No interest income - Low loss rate because they’re paying every month

Revolvers (30-40% of cardholders): - Carry a balance month to month - Pay 36-42% APR on the outstanding balance - Generate massive interest income - Higher loss rate because they’re already struggling

Here’s the arbitrage: Transactors make the card system work at scale. They spend large amounts, generate interchange on every transaction, and almost never default.

Revolvers are expensive to manage (collections, write-offs) but pay interest on balances that often exceed the original purchase price over time.

The card issuer’s job is to price cards, set limits, and manage the portfolio so that revolver interest income covers all the costs and losses.

Portfolio Math:

  • 100 cards outstanding
  • 65 transactors with ₹1,00,000 spend each = ₹65 lakh annual spend
  • 35 revolvers with average balance ₹25,000 = ₹8.75 lakh total revolving balance

Interchange on transactors: ₹65 lakh × 1.5% = ₹97,500 Interest on revolvers: ₹8.75 lakh × 36% = ₹3.15 lakh Total annual revenue: ₹4.125 lakh

Cost of funds for ₹65 lakh in transactor spend: 5% = ₹3.25 lakh (but this is mostly merchant risk, not card issuer) Cost of funds for ₹8.75 lakh in revolver balances: 5% = ₹43,750

Net available for losses, opex, profit: ₹4.125 lakh - ₹43,750 = ₹3.6875 lakh on a ₹8.75 lakh revolver book = 42% return on revolver capital.

Revolvers are a goldmine if you can collect on them.

8.3 MDR Is Just Short-Tenure Subvention

If you’ve read Chapter 5 (math of subvention), you already understand MDR. Merchant Discount Rate is the fee retailers pay card networks. It’s exactly analogous to checkout finance subvention, just with a much shorter tenure.

In checkout finance: - Tenure: 3-12 months - Subvention: 3-10%

In credit cards: - Tenure: 45 days (interest-free period) - MDR: 0.5-2%

Same mechanism, different duration.

Why MDR Is Lower Than EMI Subvention:

When a customer buys ₹30,000 on a card, the merchant gets paid in 2 days (T+2 settlement). The cardholder pays the bank in 45 days. The bank’s capital is only deployed for 45 days instead of 6 months.

Using the subvention formula from Chapter 5:

\[\text{Take%} \approx (R - F) \cdot \frac{n+1}{24} + \varepsilon + L\]

For 45-day card payment (1.5 months):

Take% ≈ (24% - 6%) × (1.5+1)/24 + 1% + 0.5% = 18% × 0.104 + 1.5% = 1.9% + 1.5% = 3.4%

So MDR on a card should be roughly 1.5-3.5% depending on the bank’s cost of funds and risk model. This lines up with actual MDR (0.5-2% is for the network fee paid by merchant’s bank; the card issuer gets a cut of this plus interchange, which totals 1.5-3%).

8.4 Why Card APR Is Higher Than Personal Loan APR

You can get a personal loan from Bajaj Finance at 12-15% APR.

You can get a credit card from the same bank at 36-42% APR.

Why is unsecured revolving credit 3x as expensive as unsecured installment credit?

Three reasons:

1. Open-Ended vs Closed-Ended

A personal loan has a fixed amount and fixed schedule. You borrow ₹1 lakh, you owe ₹1 lakh + interest. Predictable.

A credit card is open-ended. You have a ₹5 lakh limit. You might spend ₹50,000 in month 1 and ₹4 lakh in month 3. The bank doesn’t know what’s coming.

2. Duration Uncertainty

A personal loan matures in 36 months. The bank knows exactly when money is coming back.

A credit card revolver might carry their balance for 2 months or 24 months. They might clear it suddenly or slowly increase it. The duration is uncertain.

3. Behavioral Unpredictability

Someone taking a personal loan signed up for a schedule. If they miss a payment, it’s a break from normal.

A credit card revolver is already missing payments or making only minimum due. They’re signaling financial stress. The bank prices for higher probability of future stress.

The Math:

Personal loan at 15% APR: - Cost of funds: 5% - Expected loss: 2% - Opex: 1% - Capital charge: 1% - Profit margin: 6%

Credit card at 40% APR: - Cost of funds: 5% - Expected loss: 8% (revolvers have higher default rates) - Opex: 1% - Capital charge: 1% - Profit margin: 25% (to compensate for the risk and uncertain duration)

The extra 25% APR on cards reflects the extra 6% loss rate and requires fatter profit margins to absorb volatility.

8.5 Why Card Losses Are Manageable

If card APR is 40% but loss rates are only 5-8%, the math seems fragile. One bad quarter and losses eat all the profit.

Card issuers manage this through aggressive loss mitigation:

Minimum Due Enforcement: Customers must pay at least 5% of the balance every month. This ensures some cash flow even from stressed borrowers.

Dynamic Credit Limits: If you miss a payment, your limit drops immediately. If you’re delinquent for 60+ days, your card is blocked.

Early Collections: Most issuers call customers at day 1 of missed payment. Day 5, SMS reminder. Day 15, aggressive phone calls. Day 30, legal notice. By the time you’re written off at day 90+, the bank has already recovered 30-50% of the defaulted amount through collections.

Behavioral Bureau Pressure: A missed payment goes to CIBIL. Your CIBIL score tanks. This social/financial pressure makes people prioritize credit card payments over other debts.

Relationship Leverage: Card issuers know your salary, employer, and linked savings account. They can (with RBI approval) request wage garnishment or set-off from the savings account.

Taken together, these tools reduce lifetime default rates on cards to 5-8% despite 40% APR, whereas unsecured personal loans see 5-10% default at 15% APR.

The portfolio math works because: - Transactors (65%) generate profit via interchange and pay zero interest - Revolvers (35%) generate profit via interest, and most of their defaults are recovered via collections by day 90

8.6 The Complete Picture

Credit cards are priced higher than personal loans not because of interest rates alone, but because of: 1. Revolving risk (duration + amount uncertain) 2. Behavioral unpredictability (revolvers are already stressed) 3. Multiple revenue streams (interchange, interest, fees) that need to be managed together 4. A portfolio structure where transactors subsidize revolvers

The 40% APR on a card revolver is expensive, but the bank prices it this way because the alternative (refusing to lend to revolvers) would eliminate 30-40% of their customer base and the profitable interest revenue.

Note

Memorize This

Credit cards have multiple revenue streams: interchange (1-2%), interest (2-3%), fees (0.5-1.5%) = 3.5-6.5% total portfolio yield. Transactors (60-70%) subsidize revolvers (30-40%) because revolvers carry high balances at 36-42% APR. Card APR is 3x personal loan APR because duration is uncertain, behavior is unpredictable, and losses are higher. Loss mitigation (limits, collections, bureau) keeps card defaults to 5-8% despite this high leverage.
