12  Negotiation Playbook

Everything in this book comes together here. This chapter gives you tools to negotiate lending economics — whether you’re a fintech negotiating with lenders, a merchant negotiating subvention, or a product manager designing pricing.

12.1 The 30-Second Negotiation Script

You’re in a room. A lender quotes you subvention. It feels high. What do you do?

Step 1: Ask the IRR target. “What IRR are you targeting on this portfolio?”

Listen. They’ll say something like 24%. Or 28%. That’s your anchor.

Step 2: Compute the required subvention. Use the formula from Chapter 5:

\[\text{Take\%} \approx \frac{(R - F) \cdot (n + 1)}{24} + \varepsilon + L\]

Where: - \(R\) = IRR target (e.g., 24%) - \(F\) = funding cost (e.g., 10%) - \(n\) = tenure in months (e.g., 6) - \(\varepsilon\) = opex cost (e.g., 1%) - \(L\) = loss rate (e.g., 2%)

Step 3: Work the example.

Lender targets 24% IRR. 6-month tenure. No upfront float (so F = 0).

\[\text{Take\%} = \frac{(24\% - 0\%) \cdot (6 + 1)}{24} + 1\% + 2\%\] \[= \frac{24\% \times 7}{24} + 1\% + 2\%\] \[= 7\% + 1\% + 2\%\] \[= 10\%\]

But they have upfront float. Assume they deploy capital upfront for 1.7 months (the effective float on a 6-month loan). That’s \(\frac{1.7}{6} \approx 28\%\) of the funding period.

Float adjustment = 10% × 1.7 months of returns = roughly 1.7% reduction.

\[\text{Adjusted Take\%} \approx 10\% - 1.7\% \approx 8.3\%\]

Step 4: The pitch.

“You’re targeting 24% IRR. Opex is 1%, loss is 2%. That means you need ~8-9% total take on a 6-month loan. Let’s settle at 8.5%.”

Now you have a defensible number. Not arbitrary. Math-based.

Note

Lenders respect math. They don’t like being told “industry standard is 8%.” They do respect “I calculated your required take at 8.3% based on your stated IRR target and opex, and here’s why.”

12.2 Quick Reference Table

Here’s a table for 24% IRR with 1% opex, 2% loss, no float. Adjust as needed:

Tenure (months) Required Subvention (approx)
1 2-2.5%

6 8-8.5%
9 11-11.5%
12 14-15%

These are ballpark. Adjust for: - Higher opex (add 0.5-1%) - Higher loss assumption (add 0.5% per 1% loss) - Funding cost difference (adjust the F term in the formula)

12.3 Questions to Ask Lenders

When a lender pitches subvention, ask these six questions. Listen carefully to the answers:

12.3.1 1. “What is your assumed lifetime loss rate?”

  • Good answer: “2-3% for checkout finance, 1-1.5% for home loans.”
  • Red flag answer: “5% for all products” or “I don’t know, we estimate it quarterly.”
  • What to do: If they say 5% for 6-month checkout, they’re overestimating. Industry data suggests 2-3%. Push back.

12.3.2 2. “Is that realized or buffered?”

  • Good answer: “Realized. We track it monthly.”
  • Red flag answer: “Buffered. We reserve 5% on day 1.”
  • What to do: Buffering inflates opex. If they buffer high, they’re covering up for inefficiency.

12.3.3 3. “What is your cost of funds?”

  • Good answer: “10-12%, split between NCD borrowing (12%) and repo (9%).”
  • Red flag answer: “It varies” or “I can’t share that.”
  • What to do: Cost of funds is the biggest lever. If they quote 14-15%, they’re being greedy. Market rate for NBFCs is 10-12%.

12.3.4 4. “What average outstanding assumption are you using?”

  • Good answer: “Average of opening and closing, which is ~30% of loan amount for a 6-month tenure.”
  • Red flag answer: “Full loan amount for the full tenure.”
  • What to do: If they assume full amount, they’re overstating the interest they’ll earn. Challenge it.

12.3.5 5. “What IRR does this imply?”

  • Good answer: Lender can work backward from their subvention ask and confirm it drives 24% IRR (or whatever they targeted).
  • Red flag answer: “I’m not sure” or “That’s confidential.”
  • What to do: If they can’t show you the math, they’re winging it. Walk.

12.3.6 6. “What alternative deployment yields do you have?”

  • Good answer: “Personal loans at 20%, and home loans at 12%. Your 24% checkout IRR fits between high-risk and lower-risk.”
  • Red flag answer: “Checkout is our only option.”
  • What to do: Their alternatives matter. If they can’t deploy capital anywhere else, they’ll negotiate. If they have tons of high-yield opportunities, they won’t.

12.4 Red Flags in Subvention Asks

Watch for these five red flags. If you see them, the lender is either incompetent or trying to squeeze you:

12.4.1 1. Linear Math (the biggest mistake)

“You want 6 months? That’s half of 12 months. So half of 12% subvention is 6%. Here’s ₹6 lakh.”

Why it’s wrong: The formula is not linear. A 6-month loan at 24% IRR doesn’t need half the subvention of a 12-month loan. The required subvention is: - 6 months: ~8% - 12 months: ~15%

Linear thinking would predict 7.5%. Reality is higher because funding drag scales non-linearly with tenure.

12.4.2 2. Ignoring Float

“You’re deploying capital upfront. So your interest benefit is larger. But I’ll ignore that.”

Why it’s wrong: Upfront float is real value. If you deploy capital 2 months early, you earn 2 months of interest on that capital. That’s 1.5-2% reduction in required subvention. Never agree to a subvention that doesn’t account for your float.

12.4.3 3. Inflated Loss Assumptions

“I’m assuming 5% loss rate on checkout finance.”

Why it’s wrong: For 6-month checkout loans, industry loss rates are 2-3%. 5% is outlier-high. It pads opex. Push back with data.

12.4.4 4. Monthly Loss Quoting

“Our monthly loss is 3%.”

Why it’s wrong: That’s 36% annual loss! Nonsense. Losses should always be quoted as annual or as lifetime loss on the cohort. If someone quotes monthly, they’re either confused or lying.

12.4.5 5. Tenure-Flat Subvention

“We need 8% subvention for all tenures: 3 months, 6 months, 12 months.”

Why it’s wrong: 8% for 3 months is overpaying them. 8% for 12 months is underpaying them. The formula shows it should scale with tenure:

\[\text{Take\%} \propto (n + 1)\]

So subvention should increase with tenure. If a lender quotes flat subvention, they’re not thinking clearly. Or they’re using a loose all-in margin.

Important

Red flag #1 (linear math) is the most common. Most executives, even smart ones, think checkout finance subvention linearly. It’s not. The formula is non-linear. If you remember one thing from this section, remember that.

12.5 Component-Based Negotiation

Instead of haggling over one big number, break subvention into components. This clarifies where the money actually goes.

\[\text{Total Subvention} = \text{Funding Drag} + \text{Loss} + \text{Opex} + \text{Fair Margin}\]

Here are typical ranges for a 6-month tenure with 24% IRR and 10% cost of funds:

Component Low High Notes
Funding Drag 3% 3.5% (24%-10%) × 6/24, roughly
Loss 2% 3% Industry standard for checkout
Opex 0.5% 1.5% Digital lending: mostly tech, not humans
Fair Margin 1% 2% Profit for the lender
TOTAL 6.5% 10%

If a lender asks for 12%, and this table sums to 8.5%, they’re pocketing 3.5% excess margin. Call it out: “Your components add to 8.5%. You’re asking for 12%. Where does the extra 3.5% go?”

Usually, they’ll explain it’s for ops, or contingency, or “hard to quantify costs.” But now you’re negotiating on real terms, not magic numbers.

12.6 One-Page Cheat Sheet

Print this and keep it handy:

CORE FORMULA \[\text{Take\%} \approx \frac{(R - F) \cdot (n + 1)}{24} + \varepsilon + L\]

QUICK NUMBERS

For 24% IRR, 10% cost of funds, 1% opex, 2% loss: - 1 month: ~2.5% - 3 months: ~4.5% - 6 months: ~8.5% - 12 months: ~15%

8 KEY PRINCIPLES

  1. Subvention pays for: funding drag (yield gap) + losses + opex + lender margin.
  2. Funding drag scales with (tenure + 1), not tenure. Non-linear.
  3. Upfront float reduces required subvention by ~1.5-2%.
  4. Loss rate for 6-month checkout is 2-3%, not 5%.
  5. Cost of funds is the biggest lever. Push on it first.
  6. Higher opex = higher subvention. Digital platforms should have 0.5-1% opex, not 2%.
  7. “No-cost EMI” is a term for the customer. Subvention is the actual cost.
  8. If the lender can’t explain the math, walk. There are other lenders.

DECISION FRAMEWORK

Lender Asks Your Counter If They Refuse
12% on 6-month “Components add to 8.5%. What’s the 3.5% for?” Escalate to their CFO or walk.
10% on 3-month “Math says 4.5%. You’re 5.5% high.” Ask for cost of funds breakout. Are they 14-15%?
Flat 8% all tenures “8% for 3 months overpays you. Use tenure-based formula.” Offer tiered: 4% (3m), 8% (6m), 14% (12m).
5% loss assumption “Industry is 2-3%. Where’s the 5% from?” Ask for their historical data. If they won’t share, they don’t have it.

12.7 Memorize This

Subvention = Funding Gap + Loss + Opex + Margin. The formula is non-linear with tenure. Cost of funds is your leverage. If they can’t explain the math, find another lender.
