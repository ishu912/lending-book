4  How Lenders Set Their IRR Target

A lender’s required Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is not a magic number pulled from thin air. It’s the answer to a simple question: “How much return do I need to make this loan worth the risk?”

The answer is always the same: add up five things. Every rupee the merchant pays flows into one of these five buckets. This chapter shows you how.

4.1 The Five Components

When a lender decides they need, say, 18% IRR on a merchant credit product, they’re actually saying: “These five costs and profit targets add up to 18%.”

Component 1: Cost of Funds

First, the lender has to borrow money to lend to you. That costs them something.

  • A bank borrows at 4-6% (from deposits, other banks, RBI)
  • An NBFC borrows at 9-12% (from banks, bonds, commercial paper)
  • A fintech borrows at 12-16% (from banks, private lending, aggregators)

This is their baseline cost. If they lend at anything below this rate, they lose money before we even count risk or operations.

Component 2: Expected Credit Loss

Some borrowers will default. You know this. So does the lender.

Expected Loss = Probability of Default × Loss Given Default

Suppose the lender expects 3% of merchants to default. And when they default, the lender recovers 40% of the loan (sells inventory, collateral, etc). Then:

Loss Given Default = 1 - 0.40 = 60% Expected Loss = 3% × 60% = 1.8% of the loan amount

A ₹100,000 loan in this scenario costs the lender ₹1,800 in expected losses, baked into the price.

Component 3: Operating Cost

Running the lending business costs money. KYC checks. Underwriting. Servicing. Collections.

For merchant lending, this typically runs 1-3% of the loan amount annually.

A ₹100,000 loan might cost ₹1,000-3,000 to originate, service, and potentially collect on.

Component 4: Capital/Regulatory Charge

Banks must hold capital against every loan they make. An 8% capital adequacy requirement means that on a ₹1,00,000 portfolio, they must hold ₹8,000 in capital.

That capital has a cost. It needs to earn the bank’s cost of equity (typically 15-20% per year). So even though they’re only holding 8%, the cost per loan works out to roughly 1-2% IRR.

This is pure regulation. It exists to make the banking system safer. The lender passes it to you.

Component 5: Required Profit

After costs, the lender wants profit. Shareholders expect a Return on Assets (ROA) — profit as a percentage of assets — of 2-4% per year on average.

4.1.1 The Formula

\[\text{Required IRR} = \text{Cost of Funds} + \text{Expected Loss} + \text{Opex} + \text{Capital Charge} + \text{Profit Target}\]

Every percentage point in one component has to come from somewhere. If your cost of funds goes up 1%, and you don’t want to cut profit, your lending rate goes up 1%.

4.2 Why Different Lenders Need Different IRRs

Not all lenders are the same. A bank and a fintech face different costs, so they need different IRRs.

Component Bank (Secured) NBFC Consumer Finance NBFC
Cost of Funds 5% 10% 10%
Expected Loss 2% 2% 2%
Opex 1% 2% 2%
Capital Charge 1.5% 1% 1%
Profit Target 2% 3% 5-8%
Total IRR 11.5% 18% 20-23%

A bank can lend at 11.5% for secured loans because deposits are cheap. An NBFC needs 18% because borrowed money costs more. A consumer finance NBFC needs 20-23% because defaults are higher and they need fatter profit margins to survive a bad quarter.

This is why you see: - Bank personal loans at 10-12% - NBFC personal loans at 15-18% - Fintech personal loans at 18-24%

The lender’s cost structure makes the IRR, and you pay it.

4.3 The Per-Transaction vs Portfolio View

Here’s where merchants get confused. You think in terms of take-rate: the percentage you pay per transaction. The lender thinks in terms of IRR: the annual return on the entire portfolio.

These are connected by tenure. Short tenure → low take-rate. Long tenure → high take-rate.

\[\text{Take-rate %} = \frac{\text{Subvention Amount}}{\text{Transaction Value}} \times 100\]

For Bajaj-style consumer durable (CD) loans, typical take-rates are 3-8% depending on: - How long the EMI runs (3 months vs 12 months) - How risky the merchant category is - Competitive pressure in that segment

A 6-month EMI might cost the merchant 5-6% subvention. A 12-month EMI might cost 8-10%.

The lender’s 22% IRR is spread across twelve months of getting ₹1/12 of the principal back. Your 6% take-rate is what covers that spread over six months.

4.4 How to Think Like Bajaj Finance

Bajaj Finance (one of India’s largest NBFC-MFI companies) lends heavily to consumer durables and appliances. Here’s roughly how they think:

Metric Bajaj Range
Required IRR 22-30%
Expected Loss Rate 1.5-4%
Cost of Funds 7-9%
Opex 2-3%
Profit (ROA) >4%

The difference between IRR target and cost of funds (22% - 8% = 14%) has to come from subvention from the merchant, plus whatever the customer pays for processing fees, plus float income, plus down payment contributions.

If you’re a merchant and Bajaj comes to you with “no-cost EMI,” you know they’re not actually earning 22% from you alone. They’re: 1. Taking subvention from you (the biggest part) 2. Taking processing fees from the customer (1-2%) 3. Earning float on collections (1-2%) 4. Getting some margin from a lower loss rate than they modeled

It all adds up to 22% IRR.

Important

Memorize This

Required IRR = Cost of Funds + Losses + Opex + Capital Charge + Profit

Every basis point (0.01%) in your cost of funds flows directly to what the merchant must pay. If your funding cost rises 1%, you need 1% more from the merchant unless you cut something else.
