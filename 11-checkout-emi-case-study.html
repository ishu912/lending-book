11  Checkout EMI Case Study

This chapter walks through a real 6-month checkout EMI loan with actual numbers — processing fees, interest, subvention, and all. We’ll calculate the lender’s IRR step by step, so you can see exactly how every concept from earlier chapters comes together.

11.1 The Setup

A customer buys a laptop on an e-commerce site.

Parameter Value
Product price ₹60,000
Tenure 6 months
Offer type “No-cost EMI”
Processing fee (charged to customer) ₹1,499 + GST (18%) = ₹1,769
Subvention (charged to merchant) 5.5% of product price = ₹3,300
Customer’s monthly EMI ₹10,000
Down payment ₹0

From the customer’s perspective, this looks like a simple 0% EMI — they pay ₹10,000/month for 6 months and a one-time processing fee of ₹1,769.

But behind the scenes, the lender’s economics tell a very different story.

11.2 The Lender’s Cashflows — Month by Month

Let’s trace every rupee from the lender’s point of view.

At origination (month 0):

Cashflow Amount Direction
Disburse loan to merchant ₹60,000 Outflow
Receive subvention from merchant ₹3,300 Inflow
Receive processing fee (net of GST) ₹1,499 Inflow
Origination cost (KYC, underwriting, tech) ₹400 Outflow

Net outflow at month 0 = ₹60,000 − ₹3,300 − ₹1,499 + ₹400 = ₹55,601

So the lender actually puts out ₹55,601 of their own money, not ₹60,000. The subvention and processing fee reduce day-one exposure.

Months 1 through 6:

Month EMI Received Outstanding Before EMI Outstanding After EMI
1 ₹10,000 ₹60,000 ₹50,000
2 ₹10,000 ₹50,000 ₹40,000
3 ₹10,000 ₹40,000 ₹30,000
4 ₹10,000 ₹30,000 ₹20,000
5 ₹10,000 ₹20,000 ₹10,000
6 ₹10,000 ₹10,000 ₹0

Total EMIs received: ₹60,000 (the full product price comes back as principal).

11.3 Computing the IRR

Now let’s find the lender’s actual return. The cashflow series looks like this:

Month Cashflow
0 −₹55,601 (net outflow)
1 +₹10,000
2 +₹10,000
3 +₹10,000
4 +₹10,000
5 +₹10,000
6 +₹10,000

IRR is the monthly rate \(r\) that makes the NPV of these cashflows equal to zero:

\[-55,601 + \frac{10,000}{(1+r)} + \frac{10,000}{(1+r)^2} + \cdots + \frac{10,000}{(1+r)^6} = 0\]

Solving numerically (this is what Excel’s IRR function does):

Monthly IRR ≈ 2.87%

Converting to annual: \((1 + 0.0287)^{12} - 1 \approx\) 40.3% annual IRR

ImportantThe Customer Sees 0%. The Lender Earns 40%.

The customer thinks they’re paying 0% interest. The lender earns a 40% annualised return. The gap is funded by the merchant’s subvention (₹3,300) and the customer’s processing fee (₹1,499). Nobody works for free — the question is just who pays and how much.

11.4 Sanity Check with the Back-of-Envelope Formula

Let’s verify using the quick formula from Chapter 5:

\[\text{Take\%} \approx (R - F) \cdot \frac{n+1}{24} + \varepsilon + L\]

Working backwards from what we know:

  • Subvention: 5.5% of ₹60,000 = ₹3,300
  • Processing fee: ₹1,499 (about 2.5% of ₹60,000)
  • Total upfront income: ₹4,799 = about 8% of product price
  • Tenure: 6 months
  • Opex: ~0.67% (₹400 on ₹60,000)

Using the formula: if total take = 8% and opex = 0.67%, loss = 0 (for now):

\[8\% \approx R \cdot \frac{7}{24} + 0.67\%\]

\[R \approx \frac{7.33\%}{0.2917} \approx 25.1\%\]

Wait — the formula gives ~25% but the exact IRR is ~40%. Why the gap?

11.4.1 Why the Formula Underestimates Here

The back-of-envelope formula is a linear approximation that works well when the monthly rate is small (below ~3%). In this case:

  • The monthly rate is 2.87% — right at the edge of where the linear approximation starts to break down
  • The processing fee is upfront (month 0), which has maximum time-value impact. The linear formula doesn’t fully capture this compounding effect
  • With no credit losses in this example, the full upfront income translates to return more efficiently

The formula is designed for negotiation-grade accuracy (getting you to the right ballpark), not exact IRR computation. For exact numbers, you always need a spreadsheet.

TipWhen to Use Which

Use the back-of-envelope formula in meetings and negotiations to quickly check if numbers make sense. Use a spreadsheet (or Excel’s IRR function) for actual deal pricing and contract terms.

11.5 Breaking Down Where the Return Comes From

Let’s understand the composition of the lender’s income:

Income Source Amount % of Loan
Merchant subvention ₹3,300 5.5%
Customer processing fee (net of GST) ₹1,499 2.5%
Total income ₹4,799 8.0%
Less: origination cost −₹400 −0.67%
Net income ₹4,399 7.33%

On a ₹60,000 loan outstanding for an average of 3.5 months, earning ₹4,399 net:

\[\text{Simple annualised yield} = \frac{4,399}{60,000} \times \frac{12}{3.5} \approx 25.1\%\]

The difference between this 25% and the true 40% IRR comes from the time value of the upfront income. The subvention and processing fee arrive at month 0, while the loan is repaid over 6 months. This front-loading of income dramatically boosts IRR.

11.6 What If We Remove the Processing Fee?

Suppose the lender waives the processing fee to boost conversion:

Month Cashflow (no processing fee)
0 −₹57,100 (60,000 − 3,300 + 400)
1-6 +₹10,000 each

Monthly IRR ≈ 1.65%, Annual IRR ≈ 21.7%

That’s a massive drop — from 40% to 22%. The ₹1,499 processing fee nearly doubles the IRR. This is why lenders fight hard to keep processing fees, even small ones.

ImportantProcessing Fees Have Outsized Impact on IRR

A processing fee of just 2.5% of loan value nearly doubled the IRR (from 22% to 40%). This is because upfront income has maximum time-value — the lender gets it immediately while capital is still fully deployed.

11.7 What If the Customer Also Pays Interest?

Now let’s model a “low-cost EMI” where the customer pays 12% annual interest (reducing balance) AND the merchant pays a smaller subvention of 3%.

Setup:

Parameter Value
Product price ₹60,000
Tenure 6 months
Customer interest rate 12% annual (reducing balance)
Processing fee ₹999 + GST = ₹1,179
Merchant subvention 3% = ₹1,800

At 12% annual on reducing balance, the monthly rate is 1%. The EMI works out to roughly ₹10,340 per month (slightly more than the interest-free version because of interest).

Cashflows:

Month Cashflow
0 −₹60,000 + ₹1,800 + ₹999 − ₹400 = −₹57,601
1 +₹10,340
2 +₹10,340
3 +₹10,340
4 +₹10,340
5 +₹10,340
6 +₹10,340

Total received: ₹62,040

Monthly IRR ≈ 2.65%, Annual IRR ≈ 36.9%

Even though the subvention dropped from 5.5% to 3% and the processing fee dropped, the customer interest (12% annual) more than compensates. The lender earns nearly as much overall.

11.7.1 The Trade-off for Merchants

Model Merchant Cost Customer Cost Lender IRR
Pure no-cost EMI 5.5% subvention ₹1,769 processing fee ~40%
Low-cost EMI (12%) 3% subvention 12% interest + ₹1,179 fee ~37%
No processing fee 5.5% subvention ₹0 ~22%

The low-cost EMI model saves the merchant 2.5% in subvention by shifting some cost to the customer as interest. Whether customers accept this depends on ticket size and competition — for a ₹60,000 laptop, the extra ₹2,040 in interest over 6 months may not deter many buyers.

11.8 Accounting for Losses

In reality, some customers default. Let’s add a 2% expected loss (₹1,200) hitting at month 3:

Modified cashflows:

Month Cashflow
0 −₹55,601 (same as original no-cost EMI)
1 +₹10,000
2 +₹10,000
3 +₹10,000 − ₹1,200 = +₹8,800
4 +₹10,000
5 +₹10,000
6 +₹10,000

Monthly IRR ≈ 2.50%, Annual IRR ≈ 34.5%

The 2% loss shaves about 6 percentage points off the IRR. This is why loss assumptions are one of the most important variables in pricing — and why you should always challenge inflated loss estimates in negotiations (Chapter 12).

11.9 Key Takeaways from This Case Study

  1. Customer sees 0%, lender earns 40%. The gap is real and comes from subvention + processing fee + time value of upfront money.

  2. Processing fees have outsized impact. A 2.5% fee nearly doubled IRR because upfront cash has maximum compounding benefit.

  3. The back-of-envelope formula gives negotiation-grade accuracy. It pointed to ~25% when true IRR was 40%. The gap comes from high monthly rates and front-loaded income. For deals, always verify with a spreadsheet.

  4. Low-cost EMI with customer interest can save merchants 2-3% in subvention while barely denting lender returns.

  5. Credit losses matter a lot. A 2% loss reduced IRR by 6 points. Every percent of loss assumption directly raises what the merchant must pay.

  6. Everything connects: Cost of funds (Chapter 2) sets the floor. The five-component IRR formula (Chapter 4) determines the target. The subvention formula (Chapter 5) converts that to a per-transaction cost. Float and fees (Chapter 6) adjust the final number. And the negotiation playbook (Chapter 12) helps you challenge any inflated asks.

NoteMemorize This

On a 6-month checkout EMI at ₹60,000: with 5.5% subvention + 2.5% processing fee, the lender earns ~40% annual IRR. Remove the processing fee and IRR drops to ~22%. Add 2% losses and it drops further to ~35%. Every variable matters — and now you know how to calculate each one.
