2  Cost of Funds

2.1 What Is Cost of Funds?

Cost of funds is the interest rate a lender itself pays to borrow money. This is the floor. A lender can never sustainably lend below their cost of funds. If they do, they lose money on every loan.

Think of it like this. You own a shop. You buy inventory at wholesale price and sell it at retail price. The wholesale price is your cost. You can’t sell below cost and stay in business. Cost of funds is a lender’s wholesale cost.

It directly affects everything a lender does:

  • A bank with cost of funds at 5% can offer 9% loans and pocket 4%.
  • An NBFC with cost of funds at 10% needs to charge 14% just to pocket the same 4%.
  • A fintech with cost of funds at 15% needs to charge 19% for the same profit.

This is why lenders with cheaper funds dominate their markets. They can undercut competitors and still make money.

2.2 Where Different Lenders Get Their Money

Not all lenders get money the same way. The source matters enormously.

Type of Lender Funding Source Cost of Funds
Bank CASA deposits (savings & current accounts) 4-6%
Bank Term deposits (fixed deposits) 6-8%
NBFC Bank loans 8-11%
NBFC Market borrowing (bonds, CPs) 9-12%
Fintech Bank loans (smaller, harder to get) 12-16%
Fintech Investor equity Variable

Banks have a huge advantage: CASA deposits. CASA stands for Current Account and Savings Account. Customers keep money in these accounts to pay bills and spend. Banks pay these customers almost nothing in interest — typically 3-4% on savings and 0% on current accounts. Yet the bank keeps the money and lends it out at 9% or higher. The spread is massive and stable.

This is why banks dominate retail lending and keep it cheap.

NBFCs don’t have customer deposits. They have to borrow from banks or issue bonds in the market. These are expensive sources. Banks charge NBFCs 8-11% when they lend to them. The market charges 9-12% when NBFCs issue bonds. So the NBFC’s cost of funds is much higher.

Fintechs have it even harder. They’re new and small. Banks are hesitant to lend to them at cheap rates. They often borrow at 12-16%. Some raise money from equity investors, but that’s also expensive in the long run.

2.3 Why Cost of Funds Matters for Everything Else

Cost of funds is the rock bottom. It determines pricing, margins, and who can compete.

Here’s a concrete example.

A bank and an NBFC both want to offer a ₹1,00,000 personal loan with 0% EMI. To do this, they need to earn 20% IRR (the market rate for personal loans).

Bank’s math: - Cost of funds: 5% - Target IRR: 20% - Spread needed: 15% - They can comfortably charge this spread through subvention and fees.

NBFC’s math: - Cost of funds: 10% - Target IRR: 20% - Spread needed: 10% - They need to charge 5% less. But the lender (bank borrowing to them) needs to earn 10%. The customer needs a competitive product. Margins get squeezed.

The NBFC is in a bind. They can’t lower their cost of funds. They have to accept lower margins or charge higher subvention, which makes their product less competitive.

Now imagine a large bank enters checkout finance. They have: - Cost of funds: 4% - They can offer subvention that’s 6% cheaper than the NBFC.

The NBFC can’t compete on price. This is a real threat in Indian fintech. Fintechs built checkout finance when banks weren’t interested. Now that banks are interested, fintechs are losing share.

2.4 Cost of Funds and Subvention

In merchant lending, cost of funds directly limits how low subvention can go.

A lender looks at their cost of funds and says: “What’s the minimum subvention I need to accept to make a profit?”

Let’s say an NBFC’s cost of funds is 10% and operating costs are 2%. They need to earn at least 12% just to break even. So the minimum subvention they’ll accept on a ₹1,00,000 loan is about ₹12,000 (12% of ₹1,00,000). If a merchant tries to negotiate below ₹12,000, the lender walks away.

A bank’s cost of funds is 5%. Operating costs are 1%. They need 6% minimum. They can accept ₹6,000 in subvention on the same ₹1,00,000 loan. This bank can undercut the NBFC and still be profitable.

When a large bank enters merchant lending, they can accept lower subvention than NBFCs. NBFCs either drop their subvention to stay competitive (and accept lower margins) or exit the market. This is what’s happening in India right now.

2.5 The Spread

The spread (also called net interest margin) is the difference between what a lender charges and their cost of funds.

For banks: - Typical cost of funds: 5% - Typical lending rate: 9% - Spread: 4%

For NBFCs: - Typical cost of funds: 10% - Typical lending rate: 18-24% - Spread: 8-14%

For checkout finance: - The spread is often much higher because the lender gets paid upfront through subvention. - Example: Bank’s cost of funds is 5%. Customer’s stated rate is 0%. But the merchant pays 8% upfront as subvention. The effective spread is 3%.

High spreads don’t always mean high profits. The NBFC might have a 10% spread but also have 8% losses from defaults. The bank might have a 4% spread but only 0.5% losses. After losses and operating costs, the bank’s actual profit is higher.

But in general: higher spreads allow for higher margins and more room for mistakes.

TipMemorize This

Cost of funds is the single biggest driver of lending economics. A lender with cheaper funds can undercut competitors on price and still make more profit. This is why banks dominate retail lending. It’s also why new fintechs struggle — they have expensive funding and can’t compete on price.
