7  Checkout Finance & No-Cost EMI

7.1 The Checkout Finance Value Chain

Checkout finance involves multiple parties, each with different economics:

Player Role Economics
Customer Buys product, pays EMIs Pays 0% (in no-cost EMI)
Merchant Sells product Pays subvention (5–10%)
Lender (NBFC/Bank) Finances purchase Earns IRR of 20–28%
PG/Fintech Routes transaction Earns 0.5–1% routing fee
Brand Manufactures product Sometimes co-funds subvention

7.1.1 Money Flow

  1. Customer selects product (₹30,000) and chooses 6-month EMI
  2. Lender disburses ₹30,000 to merchant
  3. Merchant pays ~₹2,400 (8%) as subvention to lender
  4. Customer pays ₹5,000/month for 6 months to lender
  5. Net: merchant receives ₹27,600, lender earns ~22% IRR

7.2 Typical Costs: No-Cost EMI via Credit Cards

When no-cost EMI is offered through credit cards:

  • Standard MDR: ~1–3% of transaction value
  • Additional subvention for “no-cost” label: 3–6%+
  • Total effective cost to merchant: ~4–8%+ depending on tenure

7.2.1 What Affects the Cost

  • Tenure: Longer = higher cost (lender’s capital tied up longer)
  • Credit risk: Higher risk segment = higher cost
  • Product category: High-margin categories absorb higher cost
  • Processing fees: If bank charges customer a processing fee, merchant cost reduces
  • Bank’s internal factors: Cost of funds, float, collections, loss assumptions

7.3 Why Merchants Accept High Subvention

At first glance, paying 5–10% subvention on every sale sounds expensive. But merchants accept it because:

7.3.1 Conversion Lift

No-cost EMI can increase conversion rates by 20–40% on high-ticket items. A customer who can’t pay ₹50,000 upfront will buy it at ₹8,333/month.

7.3.2 Higher Average Order Value (AOV)

Customers upgrade when EMI is available. They buy the ₹50,000 phone instead of the ₹30,000 one when they’re paying monthly.

7.3.3 Lower Return Rates

Customers who commit to EMIs are less likely to return products. They’ve made a financing commitment.

7.3.4 Competitive Necessity

If competitors offer no-cost EMI and you don’t, you lose sales. It’s now table stakes in consumer electronics and appliances.

7.3.5 The ROI Math

If subvention is 8% but conversion increases 30%:

  • Without EMI: 100 visitors × 2% conversion × ₹30,000 = ₹60,000 revenue
  • With EMI: 100 visitors × 2.6% conversion × ₹35,000 AOV = ₹91,000 revenue
  • Subvention cost: ₹91,000 × 8% = ₹7,280
  • Net incremental revenue: ₹91,000 − ₹60,000 − ₹7,280 = ₹23,720

7.4 Minimum Ticket Size Economics

Lenders set minimum ticket sizes (often ₹3,000–₹5,000) because small loans have worse unit economics.

7.4.1 Why Small Tickets Don’t Work

Fixed costs per loan (KYC, underwriting, servicing, collections) are roughly constant regardless of loan size. On a ₹3,000 loan with 6% subvention, the lender earns only ₹180. If origination costs ₹100–150, there’s barely any margin.

7.4.2 The Minimum Ticket Formula (Conceptual)

\[\text{Min Ticket} = \frac{\text{Fixed Cost per Loan}}{\text{Net Margin \%}}\]

If fixed cost = ₹150 and net margin = 3%:

\[\text{Min Ticket} = \frac{150}{0.03} = \text{₹5,000}\]

Below this, every loan loses money for the lender.

7.5 Loss Economics in Checkout Finance

7.5.1 What “Loss %” Means

When lenders say “Loss = 3%,” they mean: 3% of original principal is expected to be lost over the full life of the loan cohort.

This is:

  • Not monthly (3% monthly would mean 36% annual — no lender survives that)
  • Not annualized like interest (it’s a one-time cohort-level estimate)
  • Lifetime and tenure-adjusted (longer tenure = higher loss %)

7.5.2 Typical Loss Rates

Product Lifetime Loss %
3-month BNPL ~4%
6-month checkout EMI ~2–3%
12-month consumer loan ~4–6%
Credit cards (SBI Cards) ~6–7% credit cost
Bajaj Finance consumer loans ~1.5–2.5%

7.5.3 How Losses Hit IRR

Losses reduce the cashflows the lender receives. But timing matters:

  • Early defaults hurt more (less principal recovered, higher loss-given-default)
  • Late defaults hurt less (most principal already repaid, discounted value is lower)

This is why short-tenure products have better loss economics — even when default rates are similar, the exposure window is shorter.

NoteMemorize This

Checkout finance earns 20–28% IRR through merchant subvention. Merchants accept 5–10% cost because of conversion lift and higher AOV. Loss % is lifetime per cohort — never monthly, never annualized.
