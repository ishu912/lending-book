7  Checkout Finance and No-Cost EMI

A customer walks into a store or lands on an e-commerce site to buy a ₹30,000 appliance. They see “No-Cost EMI Available.” They choose the EMI option. A complex machine whirs to life.

This chapter maps that machine. It shows you who makes money, who pays, and where the arbitrage is.

7.1 The Checkout Finance Value Chain

When a ₹30,000 purchase gets financed through no-cost EMI, five parties are involved:

Party Role Economics
Customer Applies for EMI, makes monthly payments Pays no interest (that’s the point). May pay processing fee (1-2%). Gets 45-90 day credit interest-free.
Merchant Sells the product, takes subvention cost Receives ₹28,200 (if 6% subvention = ₹1,800) upfront instead of ₹30,000 on day 0. ROI on ₹1,800 cost = higher conversion, lower returns.
Lender (NBFC/Bank) Gives the ₹30,000, collects EMI, absorbs defaults Targets 22-30% IRR from subvention + float + fees + lower loss rate.
PG/Fintech Connects merchant to lender, manages checkout UI, reconciliation, settlements Takes 0.5-1.5% of transaction value as a platform fee.
Brand (optional) May co-brand or subsidize the EMI offer Covers some/all of the subvention cost as a marketing expense. Samsung/LG often do this.

Money Flow on a ₹30,000 Purchase:

  • Day 0: Customer completes purchase on checkout
  • Day 0: Lender approves and disburses ₹30,000
  • Day 0: Merchant gets ₹28,200 (₹30k - ₹1,800 subvention) + PG settlement fee
  • Day 1-6: Customer pays ₹5,000 each month as EMI
  • Lender nets: ₹5k × 6 = ₹30k inflow, minus ₹28.2k net outflow to merchant, minus expected losses (~₹600), plus float income (~₹500), plus processing fees from customer (~₹600) = roughly ₹1.3k gross income on ₹28.2k deployment = ~4.6% per 6 months = ~22% IRR.

The math works because subvention (₹1,800 or 6%), float (₹500), and customer fees (₹600) add up to about ₹2,900 of lender income on a ₹30,000 deployment.

7.2 How No-Cost EMI Actually Works

“No-cost” is one of the most misleading words in lending. Someone always pays. Usually it’s the merchant. Sometimes it’s split with the customer or the brand.

Here’s the breakdown:

The Customer pays: - Processing fee (1-2% upfront) - EMI amount (₹5,000 per month for 6 months = ₹30,000 total) - No interest

The Merchant pays: - Subvention (6% in this example = ₹1,800) - Platform fees (0.5-1.5% of transaction = ₹150-450) - Net cost to merchant: 6.5-7.5% of transaction value

The Lender earns: - Subvention from merchant (₹1,800) - Processing fees from customer (₹600, net of GST) - Float income on pooled EMI and refund money (₹500) - Interest income on capital requirement (minimal, ~₹100) - Minus: expected losses (~₹600 on 2% default rate) - Total lender income: ~₹2,300 on ₹28,200 deployment

The Brand (optional): - May cover 20-50% of the subvention as a marketing cost - On this ₹30k transaction, brand might reimburse ₹300-900 to the lender or merchant - This shows up as “brand-subsidized no-cost EMI”

Net result: The merchant pays 5-8% of the transaction value. The customer pays almost nothing in rupees (just the processing fee). The lender hits 20-25% IRR. The brand (if involved) treats part of this as a customer acquisition cost.

7.3 Why Merchants Pay 5-10%

This is the key question: if a customer can buy with 0% interest, why would a merchant pay 5-10% discount to offer this?

The answer is conversion math.

Scenario A: No EMI Offering - 100 visitors - 2% conversion rate - Average order value ₹30,000 - Revenue: 100 × 2% × ₹30,000 = ₹60,000

Scenario B: With No-Cost EMI - 100 visitors - 2.6% conversion rate (30% lift is typical on high-ticket) - Average order value ₹35,000 (EMI allows higher ticket purchases) - Revenue: 100 × 2.6% × ₹35,000 = ₹91,000

Cost of EMI: - Subvention on 91k: 8% = ₹7,280

Net gain: ₹91,000 - ₹60,000 - ₹7,280 = ₹23,720

The merchant makes an extra ₹23,720 in gross profit by paying ₹7,280 in subvention.

The ROI on the subvention cost is 23,720 / 7,280 = 3.26x or 226% incremental return.

This is why EMI programs are now standard on every appliance, consumer durable, and furniture e-commerce site. The math is too good to pass up.

7.4 Minimum Ticket Size

Not every purchase should be financed. There are fixed costs per loan.

Fixed Costs per Loan: - KYC and identity verification: ₹80-150 - Credit check and underwriting: ₹40-80 - Loan servicing and monitoring: ₹50-100 per year - Collections (if default): ₹200-500

Total per-loan cost: roughly ₹300-500 upfront, ₹50-100 per year of active loan.

The Math:

A ₹3,000 loan at 6% subvention earns the lender ₹180.

If origination cost is ₹150, the lender is left with ₹30 of margin for the year. One default and they’re underwater.

A ₹5,000 loan at 6% earns ₹300. Origination cost ₹150 leaves ₹150 margin. Still tight but viable.

A ₹10,000 loan at 6% earns ₹600. After ₹150 origination, ₹450 margin covers 2-3 defaults’ cost.

Minimum Ticket Size Formula:

\[\text{Min Ticket} = \frac{\text{Fixed Origination Cost}}{\text{Take-rate%}}\]

If fixed cost is ₹300 and take-rate is 6%:

Min Ticket = 300 / 0.06 = ₹5,000

Below ₹5,000, the math breaks. Most Indian fintechs won’t finance below ₹5,000-10,000 because it’s not profitable.

This is one reason why EMI is prevalent in appliances (₹20k+) and furniture (₹30k+) but rare in apparel (₹2-5k).

7.5 Loss Economics

Defaults are the biggest unknown in merchant lending. How many customers will actually default on their EMIs?

Important: Loss rate is lifetime per cohort, not monthly or annualized.

If you originate 1,000 loans in January, track them for 12 months, and 30 default (either fully or partially), your loss rate is 3%.

Loss rates vary wildly by:

Product Typical Default Rate
Appliances (100% secured) 1-2%
Furniture 2-3%
Electronics (phone, laptop) 3-4%
Fashion/Lifestyle 4-6%
Personal loans 5-10%

Why? Appliances are hard to hide. If you default, the lender repossesses the TV and sells it. You have “skin in the game.”

Fashion items are easy to hide and have no resale value. If you buy a ₹10,000 outfit on EMI and can’t pay, the lender can’t do much. You just don’t pay.

Timing of Default Matters:

A customer who defaults in month 1 (after paying just one ₹5,000 EMI) costs the lender nearly the full ₹25,000.

A customer who defaults in month 5 (after paying ₹25,000) costs the lender only ₹5,000.

This is why default rates are tracked by cohort and vintages. A January 2024 cohort and a January 2025 cohort can have very different loss rates, even from the same merchant category.

Important

Memorize This

Merchants pay 5-10% subvention because conversion lift (20-40%) and higher AOV more than offset the cost. The ROI on subvention is typically 2-4x. Loss rates are portfolio metrics, not monthly. Appliance defaults (1-2%) are vastly lower than personal loan defaults (5-10%) because the product is collateral.
