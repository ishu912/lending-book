1  Lending Fundamentals

1.1 Typical Yields on Retail Loans (India)

Retail lending yields vary by product risk. The fundamental principle is simple: higher risk = higher yield. Here’s the landscape:

1.1.1 Secured Loans

Product Typical Yield
Home Loans 7.5–9%
Loan Against Property (LAP) 9–12%
Auto Loans (New) 8–10%
Auto Loans (Used) 12–16%
Gold Loans 10–15%

1.1.2 Unsecured / High-Yield

Product Typical Yield
Personal Loans 14–24%
Consumer Durable Finance (Checkout EMI) 22–28%
Credit Cards (Revolvers) 30–40%+ APR

Checkout finance (Bajaj, HDFC CD loans, etc.) generally earns 20–28% IRR because the ticket is small, tenure is short, collection costs are low, and subvention is upfront.

NoteWhy the Spread?

Secured loans have collateral backing (house, car, gold), so lenders accept lower returns. Unsecured loans carry full default risk, so lenders demand much higher yields to compensate.

1.2 What Is IRR in Lending?

IRR (Internal Rate of Return) is the annualized yield a lender earns considering:

  • Upfront subvention/income
  • Amortized principal repayments
  • Interest payments
  • Credit losses
  • Operating costs
  • Capital cost

In retail finance, IRR is the key metric because EMI loans decline in outstanding principal — the lender doesn’t have the full amount deployed for the full term. A simple “interest %” doesn’t capture the true yield.

Example: A lender charges 0% to the customer but earns a 6% upfront subvention on a 6-month loan. The naive view says “6% for 6 months = 12% annual.” But because the principal amortizes and subvention is received upfront, the true IRR is much higher — around 20–24%.

ImportantKey Insight

IRR already accounts for amortization and time-value. Linear formulas always overestimate the subvention needed. This is critical in negotiations.

1.3 What Is Subvention?

Subvention is the payment a merchant or brand makes to the lender to keep the customer’s EMI interest at zero.

The economics are straightforward:

  • Customer pays 0% EMI (sees no interest)
  • Bank still earns ~14–28% IRR
  • Merchant pays the difference as subvention

1.3.1 Worked Example

  • Product price: ₹30,000
  • Tenure: 6 months
  • Bank’s target IRR: 22%
  • Required subvention: ~5–6% of invoice = ₹1,500–1,800

Why do merchants agree to pay? Because no-cost EMI drives higher conversions, lower return rates, and higher average order value. The subvention is an acquisition cost that pays for itself.

1.3.2 The Bajaj Finance Model

Bajaj’s business is built around multiple revenue streams from checkout finance:

  1. Subvention revenue from merchants (primary)
  2. Processing fees from customers (sometimes)
  3. Late fees and penalties
  4. Cross-sell into loans and lifetime credit lines
  5. Float income on idle balances

1.4 MDR vs Subvention

Both MDR and subvention are costs the merchant bears. But they fund very different things:

Dimension Credit Card MDR No-Cost EMI Subvention
Cost to merchant ~2% 5–10%
Why merchant pays To accept credit cards To offer 0% EMI and sell more
Revenue recipients Banks + Networks + Acquirer Bank/NBFC primarily
Lender yield Minimal from MDR alone High: 22–28% IRR
Customer experience Pays full invoice immediately Pays over time, zero interest

The core insight: MDR is a payment processing fee. Subvention is a credit financing fee. Credit is inherently more expensive than payments because the lender’s capital is locked up for months.

1.4.1 Why MDR Is Much Lower Than Subvention

  1. Shorter duration: Credit card free period is ~45 days vs 6 months for EMI. Capital returns fast, so less subvention needed.
  2. Lower credit risk: Card spends during the free period aren’t really “loans” — loss rates on transactors are near zero.
  3. Cross-subsidy from revolvers: Banks know some users will revolve at 36–42% APR, subsidizing transactors.
  4. Cross-selling income: Cards generate fee revenue, insurance, and other product sales.
TipThink of It This Way

MDR is the subvention for the 45-day interest-free credit period on cards. It’s low because the “loan” is tiny (45 days) and banks earn from revolvers. EMI subvention is higher because the loan is 3–12 months with no revolver subsidy.

1.5 Per-Transaction Take-Rate vs Portfolio IRR

This is a common source of confusion:

  • IRR is an annualized yield on the outstanding portfolio
  • Take-rate is what the merchant sees — a % per transaction

The bridge between them:

Portfolio IRR → Bank calculates required subvention → Merchant pays per-transaction %

\[\text{Take-rate} = \frac{\text{Subvention Amount}}{\text{Transaction Value}}\]

For Bajaj-style consumer durable loans, the typical take-rate is 3–8% depending on tenure, risk, subsidy, and category.

NoteMemorize This

IRR is how the lender thinks (portfolio-level, annualized). Take-rate is how the merchant thinks (per-transaction, %). The subvention formula bridges the two.
